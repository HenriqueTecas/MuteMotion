The Problem We're Addressing

Traditional ambulance sirens create a critical dilemma for emergency medical responders.

Harmful Noise Levels

Traditional ambulance sirens exceed 120 decibels—comparable to jet engines or thunder—far beyond safety guidelines established by OSHA and WHO.

Patient Distress

Research shows siren noise increases patients' heart rates by ~30% and blood pressure by 14 mmHg, triggering stress hormones that can worsen conditions.

Silenced Sirens

1 in 7 EMS personnel report silencing sirens during critical care to avoid distressing patients, potentially compromising traffic clearing efficiency.

A Global Challenge

While EMS providers worldwide are moving toward more judicious siren use, the fundamental technology hasn't changed—creating an impossible choice between patient comfort and rapid response.

Modern research confirms that in the vast majority of cases, the stress caused by sirens can negate the small benefit of time saved or even worsen outcomes.

Evidence-Based Research

Extensive studies have documented both the benefits and drawbacks of traditional siren use.

1-4 min

A 2019 NHTSA report notes that Lights & Sirens use reduces response times by 1–4 minutes in urban settings, depending on traffic.

~30%

German clinical studies found that being transported with lights and siren caused patients' heart rates to increase by approximately 30%.

14 mmHg

The same studies showed blood pressure increases of about 14 mmHg during siren transport, along with spikes in stress hormones.

Current Industry Practices

Emergency medical services worldwide struggle with balancing rapid response and patient care.

Silencing Sirens

Many EMS providers choose to silence sirens for sensitive patients, particularly those with cardiac conditions, but this can delay transportation.

Night Protocol Adjustments

Some European cities restrict siren use at night to reduce noise pollution, but this can compromise response effectiveness.

Lights-Only Protocols

Using lights without sirens in some situations, though this often doesn't provide adequate traffic clearing capability.

No Technological Solution

Until now, there has been no technological solution that allows sirens to be effective externally while reducing interior noise.

Impact on Patient Health

The physiological effects of siren noise can be particularly harmful for certain patient populations.

Cardiac Patients

Noise-induced stress can increase cardiac workload, potentially worsening conditions for heart attack patients.

Neurological Conditions

Patients with head injuries, seizure disorders, or stroke can experience heightened sensitivity to loud noises.

Neonatal Transport

Premature infants and newborns are particularly vulnerable to the negative effects of loud noise exposure.

Mental Health Crises

Patients experiencing anxiety, PTSD, or other mental health emergencies may be adversely affected by siren noise.
